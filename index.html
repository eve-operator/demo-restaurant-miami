Our Story

From a Small Kitchen to Little Havana's Heart

In 1985, our abuela María opened a tiny 12-seat cocina on Calle Ocho with three folding tables and a charcoal grill. She didn't have a menu—just two questions: "¿Tienes hambre?" and "¿Qué te gustaría?" Every dish was made from memory, every recipe handed down from her mother in Havana.

Today, Chef Carlos (third generation) still uses those same recipes. The croquetas are still rolled by hand every morning at 6am. The mojo marinade still rests for 72 hours. The plantains are still fried in batches, never frozen.

We've grown to 120 seats, but the kitchen is still open — you can watch Carlos season the bistec, his daughter Sofia craft the Cuban coffee, and our family eat dinner together every night at 9pm (you're welcome to join).

Meet the Chef

Carlos Rodríguez
Third-Generation Keeper of the Flame

"Every morning at 6am, I'm in the kitchen. Not because I have to — because I get to. My abuela's recipes are not instructions; they're memories. The way she rolled croquetas, the sound of the plantains hitting the oil, the smell of mojo simmering for 72 hours... these are sacred."

Carlos trained at Le Cordon Bleu in Paris but returned to Miami when his mother fell ill. "I realized the most important cuisine in the world was the one I left behind in my own kitchen."

Today, he leads a team of 12, including his daughter Sofia (pastry chef) and nephew Mateo (sous chef). "We're not just cooking Cuban food — we're preserving it, one plate at a time."

"Hospitality is not a transaction. It's an offering."
— Chef Carlos
